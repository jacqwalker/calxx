Spreadsheets never disappoint

18 April 2018

Photo by Vlad Sargu on Unsplash

When a CEO’s buying frenzy is compared to a ripening teenager’s sex life, as Warren Buffet did in his recent annual letter to shareholders, you know you are onto something good. Buffet is the third richest man in the world according to Forbes, having built his fortune through his company Berkshire Hathaway. He knows a few things about buying and selling assets, or mergers and acquisitions (“M&A”) if we are to be formal, and his recent letter provides sensible advice when considering M&A deals.

M&A is as sexy as accounting gets. The hype, the thrill of the deal and the perception that high fliers work in M&A, draws hundreds of accountants to this area. For the modern-day CFO, M&A experience is a must-have. It is one of the core finance competencies. What M&A should come down to is paying a sensible price for the inherent value in the business. Too often, buyers pay over the odds only to write off the investment a couple of years later.

When Buffet considers an investment, he looks for five qualities. But one of those takes precedence over the rest, a ‘sensible purchase price’. In his recent letter to shareholders, he says that price brought a halt to many acquisition in 2017. “Why the purchasing frenzy? In part, it’s because the CEO job self-selects for “can-do” types. If Wall Street analysts or board members urge that brand of CEO to consider possible acquisitions, it’s a bit like telling your ripening teenager to be sure to have a normal sex life.”

Once a CEO hungers for a deal, he or she will never lack for forecasts that justify the purchase. Subordinates will be cheering, envisioning enlarged domains and the compensation levels that typically increase with corporate size. Investment bankers, smelling huge fees, will be applauding as well. (Don’t ask the barber whether you need a haircut.) If the historical performance of the target falls short of validating its acquisition, large “synergies” will be forecast. Spreadsheets never disappoint.”

The lesson: You can always find forecasts and synergies to justify a purchase, but are you paying a sensible price?

The thrill of the deal and the pressure to complete can overshadow the value of the transaction. The recent spate of retailers collapsing and going into administration is a stark reminder that business is hard, economic conditions are tight, and you don’t want to be paying too much.

Although today’s CFO is required to have M&A as part of their finance toolkit, perhaps the real skill comes from challenging those spreadsheets, not getting caught up in the hype and knowing what a sensible purchase price is. Warren Buffet said it best.
